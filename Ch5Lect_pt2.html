ANOVA for Difference in \(I\) Means

Data: Samples from \(I\) different groups

For each group \(i = 1, \dots, I\)

\[ \begin{array}{c|c|c} n_1 & \bar{y}_1 & s_1 \\ n_2 & \bar{y}_2 & s_2 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ n_I & \bar{y}_I & s_I \end{array} \]

Summary statistics

\[ \begin{array}{c|c|c} n & \bar{y} & s_y \end{array} \]

Combine all groups to obtain overall summaries.

Test

\[ H_0 : \mu_1 = \mu_2 = \cdots = \mu_I \]

\[ H_a : \text{At least one } \mu_i \neq \mu_j \]

One-Way ANOVA Model

For an observation from group \(i\):

\[ Y = \mu_i + \varepsilon, \qquad \varepsilon \sim N(0,\sigma_\varepsilon). \]

Interpretation:

  • \(\mu_i\) is the mean response for group \(i\).
  • \(\varepsilon\) is the within-group (random) error.

Fitted values:

  • Under \(H_0\) (all means equal): \(\hat\mu_i = \bar y\) (overall mean)
  • Under \(H_a\) (means can differ): \(\hat\mu_i = \bar y_i\) (group mean)

Alternate Form: One-Way ANOVA Model

Model

\[ Y = \mu + \alpha_i + \varepsilon \]

  • \(\mu\) = grand mean
  • \(\alpha_i\) = effect of group \(i\)
  • \(\varepsilon\) = random error

Relationship to group means

\[ \mu_i = \mu + \alpha_i \]

Estimators:

\[ \hat{\mu} = \bar{y} \qquad \hat{\alpha}_i = \bar{y}_i - \bar{y} \]

Equivalent hypothesis statements

\[ H_0:\ \mu_1 = \mu_2 = \cdots = \mu_I \]

\[ H_a:\ \text{At least one } \mu_i \neq \mu_j \]

\[ H_0:\ \alpha_1 = \alpha_2 = \cdots = \alpha_I = 0 \]

\[ H_a:\ \text{At least one } \alpha_i \neq 0 \]

MRI Overlays: A metaphor for ANOVA effects

Sum of Overlays

Start with the effects model:

\[ Y = \mu + \alpha_i + \varepsilon. \]

Decompose each response into three overlays:

\[ \text{Response} = \underbrace{\bar{y}}_{\text{Grand average}} + \underbrace{(\bar{y}_i - \bar{y})}_{\text{Treatment effect}} +\quad \underbrace{(y - \bar{y}_i)}_{\text{Residual}} \]

Sum of Overlays: FourExams

\[ y_{ij} = \bar{y} + (\bar{y}_i - \bar{y}) + (y_{ij} - \bar{y}_i), \qquad \bar{y} = 75.0 \]

\[ \begin{array}{r r r r} \text{Observed} & \text{Grand} & \text{Effect} & \text{Residual} \\ 62 & 75.0 & -3 & -10 \\ 94 & 75.0 & -3 & \hline{\} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ 87 & 75.0 & 12 & 0 \\ 95 & 75.0 & 12 & 8 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \end{array} \]

Sum of Overlays: FourExams

\[ y_{ij} = \bar{y} + (\bar{y}_i - \bar{y}) + (y_{ij} - \bar{y}_i), \qquad \bar{y} = 75.0 \]

\[ \begin{array}{l r r r r} & \text{Observed} & \text{Grand} & \text{Effect} & \text{Residual} \\[4pt] \text{Exam \#1} & 62 & 75.0 & -3 & -10 \\ \text{Exam \#1} & 94 & 75.0 & -3 & \underline{\hspace{2.5cm}} \\ & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\[6pt] \text{Exam \#2} & 87 & 75.0 & 12 & 0 \\ \text{Exam \#2} & 95 & 75.0 & 12 & \underline{\hspace{2.5cm}} \\ & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \end{array} \]

Partitioning Variability

Big idea

\[ \textbf{Data} = \textbf{Model} + \textbf{Error} \]

For a one-way ANOVA:

  • Total variation in the response
  • equals
  • Variation explained by group differences
  • plus
  • Unexplained variation (residuals)

Question

How do we measure these sources of variability?

Partitioning Variability for a One-Way ANOVA

From observations:

\[ y_{ij} = \bar{y} + (\bar{y}_i - \bar{y}) + (y_{ij} - \bar{y}_i) \]

Square and sum over all observations:

\[ \sum (y_{ij} - \bar{y})^2 = \sum (\bar{y}_i - \bar{y})^2 + \sum (y_{ij} - \bar{y}_i)^2 \]

  • Left: Total variation
  • First term: Between-group variation (treatment effects)
  • Second term: Within-group variation (residuals)

In ANOVA notation:

\[ \text{SSTotal} = \text{SSGroups} + \text{SSE} \]

ANOVA Table (for \(I\) Group Means)

Hypotheses:

\[ H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2 = \cdots = \mu_I \qquad\text{vs.}\qquad H_a: \text{some } \mu_i \ne \mu_j. \]

Source d.f. Sum of Squares Mean Square \(F\)
Groups \(I-1\) \(SS_{\text{Groups}}\) \(MS_{\text{Groups}} = \dfrac{SS_{\text{Groups}}}{I-1}\) \(\dfrac{MS_{\text{Groups}}}{MSE}\)
Error \(n-I\) \(SSE\) \(MSE = \dfrac{SSE}{n-I}\)
Total \(n-1\) \(SS_{\text{Total}}\)

Large \(F\) indicates between-group variability is large relative to within-group variability.

One-Way ANOVA in R

factor() is needed to treat the numeric Exam variable as categories.

FourExamsData <- read.csv("https://people.kzoo.edu/enordmoe/math360/FourExamsData.csv")
ExamAnova <- lm(Grade ~ factor(Exam), data = FourExamsData)
anova(ExamAnova)

  • If \(F \approx 1\), the group means are not very different relative to the variation within groups.

  • The F-ratio compares between-group variability to within-group variability.

Summary

  • We compare several group means using a single statistical model.

  • Each observation is viewed as:
    overall average + group effect + random noise.

  • Total variability in the data splits into:
    between-group variation and within-group variation.

  • The F-statistic asks:
    Are group differences large relative to ordinary random variation?
