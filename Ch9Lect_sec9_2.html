Overview

In this section we focus on careful interpretation of the logistic slope coefficient in the context of odds and odds ratios.

Proportions and Odds

Recall: A goal of logistic regression is to model

\[ \pi = P(\text{Yes}) \]

the proportion of ‚Äúyes‚Äù or ‚Äúsuccess‚Äù outcomes for different values of a predictor.

The Logit Form of the Model

\[ \log\!\left(\frac{\pi}{1-\pi}\right) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x \]

The log of the odds is a linear function of \(x\)?

Where Does This Come From?

The model is built from odds:

\[ \text{odds} = \frac{\pi}{1-\pi} = \frac{P(\text{Yes})}{P(\text{No})} \]

Taking the logarithm gives the log-odds (logit).

\[ \log\!\left(\frac{\pi}{1-\pi}\right) \]

Odds Ratio

A common way to compare two groups is to examine
the ratio of their odds.


Definition

\[ \text{Odds Ratio} = OR = \frac{\text{Odds}_1}{\text{Odds}_2} \]

\[ = \frac{\dfrac{P_1(\text{Yes})}{P_1(\text{No})}} {\dfrac{P_2(\text{Yes})}{P_2(\text{No})}} \]

Binary Explanatory Variable

Example: Exposure to a Risk Factor

  • Group 1: Exposed to a risk (e.g., smokers)
  • Group 2: Not exposed (non-smokers)

How to Think About Odds (Risk Context)

Example: Exposure to a Risk Factor

Suppose

  • \(P_1(\text{disease}) = 0.30\)
  • \(P_2(\text{disease}) = 0.10\)

Then the odds are:

  • \(\text{Odds}_1 = \frac{0.30}{0.70} = 0.43\)
  • \(\text{Odds}_2 = \frac{0.10}{0.90} = 0.11\)

So the odds ratio is

\[ OR = \frac{0.43}{0.11} \approx 3.9 \]

Interpretation:

The exposed group has about 4 times the odds of getting the disease compared to the unexposed group.

Interpreting the Odds Ratio

  • \(OR = 1\quad\Rightarrow\) no association
  • \(OR > 1\quad\Rightarrow\) higher odds in Group 1 (more likely)
  • \(OR < 1\quad\Rightarrow\) lower odds in Group 1 (less likely)

Example: TMS for Migraines üß†

  • Response variable: Pain-free? (Yes/No)

Step 1: Compute Odds

For TMS:

\[ \text{odds}_{\text{TMS}} =\frac{39/100}{61/100} =\underline{\hspace{5cm}} \]

For Placebo:

\[ \text{odds}_{\text{Placebo}} = \frac{22/100}{78/100} =\underline{\hspace{5cm}} \]

\[ OR = \frac{0.639}{0.282} =\underline{\hspace{5cm}} \]

The odds of being pain-free are \(=\underline{\hspace{5cm}}\) times higher with TMS than with placebo.

Interpreting ‚ÄúSlope‚Äù Using the Odds Ratio

\[ \log(\text{odds}) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x \]

Exponentiating both sides:

\[ \text{odds} = e^{\beta_0 + \beta_1 x} \]

What happens when we increase \(x\) by 1?

\[ e^{\beta_0 + \beta_1(x+1)} = e^{\beta_0 + \beta_1 x} \, e^{\beta_1} \]

When \(x\) increases by 1, the odds are multiplied by

\[ e^{\beta_1} \]

(the odds ratio).

Example (Putts)

The odds of making a putt are multiplied by the factor

\[ e^{-0.566} = 0.568 \]

for every additional foot of length.

Summary

  • Logistic regression models log-odds as a linear function of \(x:\) \[ \log\!\left(\frac{\pi}{1-\pi}\right) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x \]

  • The slope \(\beta_1\) is interpreted through the odds ratio: \[ OR = e^{\beta_1} \]

  • A one-unit increase in \(x\) multiplies the odds by \(e^{\beta_1}\).
